What I Do

Most execution problems aren't about effort.
They're about clarity.

Teams are usually working hard. What's missing is a clear priority order, defined ownership, and a way for leadership to see what's actually happening. That's the gap I close.

01

I make the hard prioritization calls happen.

Most leaders know the portfolio is overloaded. What they need is someone who will put everything on the table and force a real decision. Not a polite compromise that leaves 30 things in flight. I do that. The result is a plan teams can actually execute — not one that falls apart in week three.

02

I build the model that makes cross-functional delivery work.

When engineering, product, and the business each define done differently, nothing ships cleanly. I establish shared ownership, a clear cadence, and a decision-making process everyone can work within. I've done this in 90-day turnarounds and in multi-year programs. The approach is the same either way.

03

I give leadership an honest picture of what's at risk.

Most status reporting defaults to optimism. Green until it's red. I build reporting that reflects reality. Concise enough for an executive to read in five minutes. Specific enough to make a decision from. When something is heading toward failure, I say so — early, clearly, and with a path forward.

How I Work

Practical. Direct.
No theory, no fluff.

Prioritization

Start with priorities.

Before anything else, I get the full list out on the table and work with leadership to sequence it against real capacity. If everything is a priority, we have a conversation until it isn't.

Accountability

Make ownership explicit.

Every piece of work has a name attached to it. Not a team, not a function — a person. Ambiguous ownership is where programs go to die.

Alignment

Build a rhythm.

I establish a simple operating cadence — the right meetings, the right frequency, the right people in the room. Consistent touchpoints prevent small problems from becoming big ones.

Reporting

Keep reporting honest.

Status should reflect reality, not optimism. I build lightweight reporting that tells leadership what's actually happening — including the things nobody wants to say out loud.

Risk

Surface risks early.

I'd rather have an uncomfortable conversation in week two than a crisis in week eight. Risks get named, owned, and tracked — not buried in a deck nobody reads.

Delivery

Remove blockers fast.

When something is slowing a team down, I treat it as urgent. Blockers that sit unresolved kill momentum. I stay close enough to the work to catch them early.

Structure

Cut what isn't working.

If a process, meeting, or report isn't helping the team deliver, I remove it. Structure should accelerate work, not add to it.

About

20 years. Mostly fixing things.

I've spent 20 years leading programs and building PMOs where execution actually matters. FinTech, regulated banking, enterprise SaaS. Places where a missed deadline or a broken process has real consequences.

I get called in when things are off track. Maybe a program is behind and nobody wants to say it out loud. Maybe the portfolio has 30 priorities and no real plan. I come in, figure out what's actually happening, and build the structure to move forward.

At First Tech Federal Credit Union, I inherited a fraud detection program with technical debt, unresolved vendor dependencies, and a hard regulatory deadline. One shot. I restructured the delivery model, put the right people on point, and we shipped. The platform prevented over $40 million in fraud losses and won a 2025 Banking Tech Award.

I'm not a process-for-process's-sake person. The frameworks I build are meant to get out of the way and let good teams move fast. If something isn't helping the team deliver, I cut it.

At this stage of my career, I want to do this work in a space I actually care about. I'm drawn to collectibles, games, and the hobby space — where the community is passionate, the products matter, and execution quality is visible.
